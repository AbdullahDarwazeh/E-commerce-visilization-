Introduction¶

Dataset: eCommerce purchase history from electronics store

In today's competitive online landscape, ecommerce stores are facing the challenge of decreasing profit margins due to rising advertisement costs and difficulty in acquiring new customers. After all, as more and more competitors enter the market, the cost of online advertisement skyrockets, thus making it harder and more costly for ecommerce brands to acquire new customers and ultimately squeezing their profit margins. Now, one possible solution to this problem is to focus on generating repeat orders from already existing customers. After all, acquiring a new customer is way more costly tha getting a current customer to buy again (Reichheld & Schefter, 2000). To achieve this, ecommerce brands can analyze their customers' purchasing behavior and come up with targeted upselling and cross-selling recommendations. This would make advertising campaigns more effective and drive profitability upwards.

In this data mining project, we aim to look for purchasing patterns in the behavior of the customers of a ecommerce electronics store. Our goal is to help the company generate more repeat purchases by providing personalized product recommendations to their customers. We plan to achieve this by analyzing purchasing and website usage data to identify patterns that can possibly lead to personalized recommendations. By doing so, we aim to provide ecommerce stores with actionable insights that can drive sales, improve customer repeat purchase, and ultimately increase revenue.

However, to make this project applicable to real-world scenarios, we must consider certain factors. For instance, most remarketing campaigns are run through social media platforms, such as Instagram or YouTube, which have limited and costly ad space. Therefore, we cannot generate many recommendations, and we must focus on generating recommendations that are most likely to be effective from a return on investment perspective. Moreover, considering the human-in-the-loop nature of these ad platforms, explainability is a must for our project. So, by providing clear and explainable insights into the behavior of our customer base, we hope to find out into why certain upselling strategies could work and how they can be improved.

In summary, our hope is that our findings will help ecommerce stores improve customer retention, increase revenue, and ultimately become more competitive in the online marketplace by leveraging data mining techniques.

Association Pattern Mining¶

Association pattern mining is a data mining technique that involves discovering relationships or patterns among items in a dataset. Specifically, it identifies the co-occurrence of items and analyzes the strength of the relationship between them.

And, in the context of our project, we will use this data mining technique to identify items that are frequently purchased together and then create potential product recommendations to be marketed to the existing customer base via online ads.

As the online advertising landscape involves a human in the loop, often referred to as the media buyer, it is crucial to ensure explainability of the model. The rules and product recommendations generated by this data mining technique must be easy to explain to any non-technical stakeholder, otherwise, the insights gathered from it will be impractical and useless.

For this reason, the chosen data mining technique was the association rule mining with the Apriori algorithm, since its insights can easily be explained to non-technical stakeholders by using "if-then" rules (Brin et al., 1997).

Furthermore, in order to ensure that the model can be easily explained to the media buyer, this mining task will focus solely on generating association rules between categories (which are presented in a human-readable format in this dataset, unlike the product_ids which are encoded). Another advantage of this approach is that since there are fewer unique categories than unique products, the mining process will require way less computational resources and time.

On top of that, working with categories provides more general insights, allowing media buyers and other business stakeholders to have more flexibility in deciding which types of products to promote to their existing customer base based on their business goals.

In summary, this data mining technique aims to help us to understand the purchasing behavior of our customers better, and create targeted and effective data-driven product recommendations to drive sales and growth for the ecommerce store.

Now, for the sake of this project, we will only consider itemsets that have a minimum support of 0.01, meaning itemsets that appear at least 1% of the time in the entire dataset. Doing so will help us generate more meaningful and practical association rules while also saving computational resources. (Aggarwal & Sindhu, 2015)

# Generate frequent itemsets with a minimum support of 0.01
frequent_itemsets = apriori(te_df, min_support=0.01, use_colnames=True)

As we have emphasized repeatedly, interpretability of the model by humans is crucial, and therefore, we will use the lift association rule as our guiding metric. The lift can be easily interpreted by a potential media buyer as an "if-then" rule, such as: "if the antecedents of product A are purchased, then the consequents have a higher likelihood of being purchased as well."

And since ad space is costly and limited, a key constraint we have to consider in this project is maximizing the effectiveness of our product recommendations. To achieve this, we will only recommend products that have at least twice the likelihood of being purchased based on our association rule mining results. This will help us prioritize the most relevant and profitable products to promote to our existing customer base, while minimizing the cost and resources spent on ineffective ad campaigns.

Evaluating¶

Of course, evaluating the data mining algorithm we have just created is of utmost importance; after all, while it has the potential to generate a lot of orders for the company, it could potentially cost thousands of dollars if it goes wrong.

Now, since evaluating data mining tasks is not straightforward, we will follow the approach outlined by Aggarwal (2015). That is, we will transform the insights we gathered from the Apriori algorithm into a small and simple recommender system, which will work as a classification model to predict whether a customer is likely to make a purchase or not. But, before we can define this problem as a binary classification problem and choose a suitable metric to evaluate it, we must first get clear on the constraints of this project that we have to work with.

First off, since this recommender system would potentially be used by a media buyer someone with no data science skills we will aim for explainability of the model above all else.

What's more, since this is a binary classification problem, 0 will be assigned in the case of a customer who has not bought any products on the recommended category, and 1 will be assigned if the customer bought a product in the recommended category. The data used to train the model will be all of the data from before September, and the testing set will be data starting from September.

Finally, to evaluate the accuracy of the model, we will take a look at the precision, recall and F1-score, since these measures can be closely related to the return on investment (ROI) of an advertisement campaign.

Of course, force-fitting a classification task into a data mining task is not always easy and straightforward and comes with its own set of limitations, which will be discussed later in this notebook. Having said that, let us now create our recommendations engine:

recommendations = []

However, we must also assess the accuracy of this model, since - more than explainability - effectiveness and return on investment are the key metrics for determining success in marketing campaigns. To do this, we will take a look at three highly used accuracy score metrics:

  • Precision: Measures the percentage of correctly predicted positive cases out of all the positive cases predicted. In the context of our project precision is important because it assess how accurately the ads are shown to the customers who are most likely to make a purchase.
  • Recall: Measures the percentage of true positive cases (i.e., correctly identified purchasers) out of all the actual positive cases. In our context, it would measure how many of the actual purchases were correctly identified by the model.
  • F1-score: Measured as the harmonic mean of precision and recall, thus providing a single score that summarizes the balance between the two metrics.

    for i in range(len(y_true)):
        tp += len(set(y_true[i]).intersection(set(y_pred[i])))
        fp += len(set(y_pred[i])) - len(set(y_true[i]).intersection(set(y_pred[i])))
        fn += len(set(y_true[i])) - len(set(y_true[i]).intersection(set(y_pred[i])))
    precision = tp / (tp + fp)
    recall = tp / (tp + fn)
    f1 = 2 * precision * recall / (precision + recall)
    return precision, recall, f1

# Compute F1 score
test_users = test_df.apply(lambda x: list(x[x==True].index), axis=1)
precision, recall, f1 = f1_score(test_users.values, recommendations)

print(f'Precision: {precision}\nRecall: {recall}\nF1: {f1}')

    As we can see, all of the models scored fairly poorly on the three metrics. However, we do not have enough information to say with precision if this model would be useful in an ads targeting use case. After all, the profitability of an ads campaign is highly dependent on the cost of advertisement and on the margin of the products sold. So, even with a fairly low rate of true positives, a particular campaign could be profitable depending on these two other variables (Both of which would have to be optimized by the media buyer).

    On top of that, it is worth noting that - as we currently modelled this system - this binary classification model was evaluated by its capacity to predict if a customer ended up buying a given product category on their own or not. In order words, (as it is right now) it simply acted as a predictor (since recommendations didn't influence customer behavior in the testing set), and these evaluation metrics represent that.

    Therefore, if such a recommender system was to be implemented to make real-time recommendations, we would expect its accuracy scores to increase, and therefore they would have to be closely monitored. After all, marketing recommended products to these customers on an ad platform will most likely drive their behavior in the direction of buying said items, thus driving accuracy metrics upwards.

    Lastly, another great improvement that could be made to the model is to make predictions and recommendations based on particular items (instead of broad categories) and set a minimum threshold for their price-tag. After all, since online ads targeting is costly, prices have to be recouped fast. And even if the product recommendations are not very accurate, a high-ticket sale can easily recoup losses caused by bad recommendations and ultimately make the model profitable for the business.

    All in all, given that the model has ample room for improvement, the business has ample room to increase the number of repeat orders by customers, and that low to mild accuracy is already enough for the model to be profitable (of course, provided that the recommended products have high margins) this recommender prototype based on insights gathered by the data mining process could be worth deploying in a real use case scenario.
